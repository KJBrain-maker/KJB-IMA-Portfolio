Sensor Walk: Hell's Kitchen

Course — Interface Labs Completed — July 13, 2026 Tags — Design

With Ajay

Living on 42nd Street between 11th and 12th Avenue, I walk through Hell's Kitchen almost every day. I never realized how many sensors, while walking my dog, we interact with until I intentionally started looking.

The most obvious are security cameras on apartment buildings, businesses, garages, and construction sites. Most don't announce themselves beyond simply being visible, and there's no indication they've "seen" me—I just assume they're constantly recording.

Crosswalk buttons are one of the few sensors that give immediate feedback. After pressing the button, a light or audible signal lets me know it registered my request. Automatic doors are similar: the door opening is confirmation that the motion sensor detected me.

I also noticed motion sensors in stores, elevators with weight and door sensors, and countless vehicles and buses relying on cameras and proximity sensors.

The biggest takeaway was realizing how invisible most of these systems have become. I probably pass dozens of sensors every day without thinking about them. Once I started paying attention, it became clear that walking through New York means constantly interacting with technology that's quietly sensing my presence, whether I notice it or not.

Oh! My fob! I have a fob that I use to get up my elevator and you press it against the pad near the number pad.
