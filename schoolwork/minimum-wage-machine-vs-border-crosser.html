Cranking, Crossing, and Getting Caught: Minimum Wage Machine vs. Border Crossers

Course — Interface Lab Tags — Design

For this lab I'm comparing two pieces that couldn't look more different but both turn an abstract policy into something you feel in your body: Blake Fall-Conroy's Minimum Wage Machine and Chico MacMurtrie / Amorphic Robot Works' Border Crossers.

The Minimum Wage Machine

You turn a hand crank, and as long as you keep turning, a penny drops out every few seconds, timed to the local minimum wage. Stop cranking, stop getting paid. Fall-Conroy built the first version in 2008 from an antique 1913 change sorter, a salvaged printer motor, and a BASIC Stamp microcontroller: the crank spins the sorter platter and powers the electronics, a timer counts down, and a stepper motor releases exactly one penny at a time into a plexiglas box. It can be reprogrammed as wages change.

What gets me is how literal it is. No symbolism, just an exact, real-time payout of what the law says your hour is worth. Fall-Conroy has said he likes making objects that feel practical but carry real depth, and this is that instinct distilled: he was inspired by a college job earning $6.50 an hour at a gas station, where he had plenty of time to calculate seconds per penny. Most people crank for under a minute before quitting, which is the whole point. It's participatory in the most basic sense: you supply labor, the machine supplies a laughably small reward, and you decide how long you can stand it.

Border Crossers

Border Crossers is a series of massive inflatable robotic sculptures, built from high tensile fabric, that rise several stories on one side of the US–Mexico border and arc over the wall to touch down on the other. They're pneumatic, inflating to hold rigid shape during choreographed performances and deflating after, activated by teams working on both sides at once. MacMurtrie has been developing these since around 2016 on a Guggenheim Fellowship, with student teams (including a 40-foot build at the University of Michigan) and collaborators at UT El Paso's Rubin Center. His goal is six robots total, three launched from each side.

MacMurtrie grew up in Bisbee, Arizona, in an Irish-Mexican household, going to school in the border town of Naco, where friends crossed through a hole in the chain-link fence. That background matters: this isn't an outsider's statement, it's someone building a physical, temporary bridge out of the exact landscape he grew up in. He's described wanting the soft fabric to counter "the harshness of this rusty wall," and the piece encourages collective witnessing — a symbolic suspension of a boundary built specifically to prevent connection.

Comparing the two

Technically they share almost nothing: a hand crank and stepper motor versus pneumatics and high tensile fabric surviving actual desert wind. But conceptually they're doing the same move, just at opposite scales. Fall-Conroy compresses an economic argument into one repetitive motion you feel in your hand. MacMurtrie inflates a symbolic gesture into something monumental, visible from two countries at once. The Minimum Wage Machine is meant to be endured alone, and its "success" is watching people give up. Border Crossers is meant to be shared, and its success is the crossing itself.

Why these, and where JR fits in

I picked Fall-Conroy because his instinct to make objects that feel practical but carry real weight is exactly the design philosophy I want to bring to my own interactive work. I picked Border Crossers partly because of my own feelings about the wall and US immigration policy; I appreciate art that takes a genuinely sociological look at where our systems fail people without turning into a lecture.

That's also why JR's Kikito installation keeps coming to mind, even though it's not one of my official picks. In 2017 in Tecate, JR built a massive image of a toddler named Kikito peeking playfully over the border wall, designed to be seen properly only from the US side, which is its own quiet argument since Kikito's family couldn't legally cross to see it that way. On the final day, a table stretched across both sides of the fence for a shared picnic, mariachi band split in half. It's not interactive the way my two picks are, but it's doing the same emotional work: making a bureaucratic boundary suddenly physical and impossible to ignore.

All three are asking the same question in different registers: what does it feel like to encounter a system — wage law or border wall — with your body instead of just your opinions. That's the thread I want to keep pulling on in this class: interfaces that make you feel a real world condition in your hands, your patience, or your sense of scale.
