Concepts, Culture & Communication · Week 1

Katja Kwastek References: Lynn Hershman Leeson

Course — Concepts, Culture & Communication Tags — Research

Lynn Hershman Leeson is a multi-media artist and filmmaker who's spent over fifty years asking what happens to identity once a machine gets involved in representing it. Her work moves through performance, video, robotics, and AI (way before her time!) but the constant is that the viewer is never just a viewer. Her interests include feminism, race, surveillance, and artificial intelligence and identity theft through algorithms and data tracking.

In 1998, working with a team of eighteen programmers, she built "Agent Ruby," an AI chatbot with a moving female face that lived permanently online. Ruby remembered your questions, searched the web to learn, and her "moods" shifted with site traffic. She wasn't a cool trick — she was a genuine artificial intelligence, built years before Siri or Alexa existed, designed specifically to expose why tech companies default to giving AI a woman's voice and face, which I find extremely interesting. The gendering of service objects and other tools has always fascinated me…why do we see some things as "male" and some as "female"? That's a question she was asking early on in the new age of technology and human connection.

I also chose Ruby because she's the clearest version of something I already believed before I had a name for it: art should make you do something. I've always said you should walk into a theatre one way and walk out another. You enter as one version of yourself and leave as something else — new questions, interrogations, or moments you want to reflect on and/or change are possible in those spaces, I believe. I always saw the theatre as a sanctuary in a way, a place you enter and, if you're really listening, you may find profound change or healing. Ruby does exactly that — through "conversation" instead of a stage, you don't observe her, you talk to her, and the interaction itself is the artwork.

If I re-created Ruby today, I'd build her less as a face and more as a space. Perhaps an installation where your conversation with an AI is projected as light or sound that physically shifts the room around you, and the space somehow holds onto a piece of your interaction with it — how it "feels" in some representation of the mark you left on it, and others can see a glimmer of theirs even if they don't notice it exactly. So the "leaving different than you entered" is literal, not just emotional, and it's reciprocal. I'd keep Hershman Leeson's core premise — using AI to interrogate gender and identity — but push it toward the audience's own body in the room, not just their screen, leaving a mark on its own space in some way.

Would it still be categorized as interactive media art? Yes, I think so. Arguably even more so, since it would require physical presence, movement, and exchange.

Lynn Hershman Leeson (1941–present)
